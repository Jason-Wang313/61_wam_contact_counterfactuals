\begin{abstract}
Contact-rich manipulation is difficult because a single executed trajectory hides many physically plausible alternatives: the object may be heavier, slipperier, or closer to a different contact mode than the robot's nominal model assumes. This paper rebuilds a previously synthetic idea into a real MuJoCo evidence package for branch-structured world-action planning. We evaluate adaptive branch MPC on a planar contact-pushing task with hidden mass and friction, paired tasks across methods, five stress splits, five seeds, 80 episodes per split/method, confidence intervals, baselines, and ablations. The result is informative but not submission-ready: branch MPC improves over random and nominal single-branch MPC, but it mostly ties ensemble MPC and does not consistently beat robust worst-case MPC. Low-friction performance remains far below an oracle hidden-parameter planner. The honest terminal decision is \textbf{STRONG\_REVISE}, not ICLR-main readiness. Submission-hardening version: v4.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}
World-action models for robot manipulation often learn from what happened after an action. In contact-rich settings, this can collapse the very uncertainty a planner needs: two contact modes may look similar before contact, but require different pushes. The research bet behind WAM Contact Counterfactuals is that unobserved physical alternatives should remain explicit until contact observations update them.

The v3 version of this paper was an archive memo because the evidence was synthetic. The v4 rebuild replaces that scaffold with a MuJoCo benchmark and implemented planners. The rebuild is deliberately gate-oriented: if branch counterfactuals do not beat strong baselines, the paper should not be submitted as an ICLR-main paper.

\section{Benchmark}
We instantiate a planar MuJoCo contact-pushing task. A spherical pusher moves a cylindrical puck toward a target on a plane. Hidden physical parameters are puck mass and contact friction. The planner observes puck pose, chooses a sequence of push actions, and receives success if the final puck position is within 7.5 cm of the target.

\paragraph{Stress splits.}
We evaluate five splits: nominal, low friction, high friction, heavy object, and combined shift. Combined shift includes mass/friction shifts, target displacement, observation noise, and actuation noise. Every method is evaluated on the same split/seed/episode tasks.

\paragraph{Methods.}
The proposed method maintains a branch library over hidden mass/friction pairs, plans under the weighted branch prediction, and updates branch weights after each observed contact. Baselines are random push, nominal single-branch MPC, ensemble mean MPC, robust worst-case MPC, and an oracle hidden-parameter MPC planner. All non-random planners use the same action candidate set and three-push budget.

\paragraph{Rigor.}
The main run uses 5 seeds, 16 episodes per seed/split/method, for 80 episodes per split/method and 2,400 main rows. Ablations are run on the combined-shift split for 480 additional rows. We report mean success, final distance, normalized progress, and 95 percent confidence intervals.

\section{Paired Comparisons}
The paired analysis is stricter than comparing aggregate means because it evaluates methods on identical hidden-physics tasks. Branch MPC beats random by large margins on every split. Against nominal MPC, the largest gains are high friction (+0.238 success) and nominal (+0.100 success). Against robust MPC, the story is mixed: branch gains on nominal and heavy object, but loses on high friction and low friction. Against ensemble MPC, branch is effectively tied on nominal, low friction, high friction, and heavy object, and loses on combined shift.

This is the key reason the paper cannot be called submission-ready. The benchmark validates that the code is real and the hypothesis is testable, but it does not validate the mechanism as a clear ICLR-main contribution.

\section{Prior-Work Pressure}
The local hostile review set is crowded with contact-rich manipulation, visuo-tactile world models, force-aware VLA systems, diffusion policies, and world-action models \citep{prior1,prior2,prior3,prior4,prior5,prior6}. These works make it unsafe to claim novelty from generic uncertainty, data augmentation, or a planner wrapped around a model. The remaining viable boundary is narrower: branch structure must produce a measurable planning advantage under contact-mode ambiguity. The v4 results do not yet establish that boundary.

\section{Limitations and Required Revision}
The benchmark is real but custom; it is not a public manipulation benchmark or hardware validation. The branch library is handcrafted, not learned. The posterior update is simple and may under-identify friction in low-friction contact. Baselines are implemented MPC variants but not modern learned diffusion or visuo-tactile policies. Related work is still based mainly on local pool artifacts rather than a manual full-paper synthesis.

To become a plausible ICLR-main submission, the project needs learned branch inference, stronger external baselines, hardware or public benchmark validation, qualitative rollout analysis, and statistically significant gains over ensemble and robust MPC.

\section{Gate Decision}
The terminal decision is \textbf{STRONG\_REVISE}. The v4 rebuild rescues the paper from a synthetic archive by adding real MuJoCo evidence and reproducible experiments. It does not rescue the paper into ICLR-main readiness because the central mechanism is not yet decisively better than strong baselines.
